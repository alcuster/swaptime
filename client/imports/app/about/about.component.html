How to use

  • 1. Post. Create a listing by clicking "Submit" in the menu on the left. You can either request a course or offer one of your own.
  • 2. Browse. Peruse other users' listings on the "All Listings" page, and if something catches your eye, click on the listing. On the next page, you can start a chat thread with that user.
  • 3. Trade. Using the in-app messaging system, coordinate your trade. The safest way to conduct a course swap is for both parties to visit the Registrar's office.
  • 4. Cleanup. When your trade is complete, please delete the listing (or, if it belongs to another user, request that he/she do so).

SSO (Facebook) login

A motivating research question for this project is how users develop trust in other users online, and whether that trust can be 'imported' into unfamiliar sites using single-sign-on services (SSOs).

In an attempt to approach that question, I offer you, the user, the ability to log in with your Facebook account. Choosing this option will attach your Facebook profile picture to listings you make.

Why?

The intent here is to help users make informed decisions about which users to contact. You only need a @davidson.edu email to sign up for and use Swaptime, but other users may be more likely to contact you if they can verify your identity by seeing your name and profile picture, and vice versa.

Permissions

When you choose to log in with Facebook, Swaptime requests the bare minimum permissions from your Facebook profile. Specifically, Swaptime requests the 'public_profile' and 'email' permissions from your account. Info on these and other permissions can be reviewed at Facebook's developer wiki here.

Using these permissions, I store your email address (so I can get in touch with you if necessary - no spam or email lists) and your name and profile picture (for attaching to your listings).

Finer-grained SSO Permissions

Since Swaptime only benefits from storing your name and profile picture, I don't want or need access to other parts of your Facebook profile (e.g. your friends list, cover photo, or age range). Unfortunately, Facebook only gives access to profile pictures under a larger 'public_profile' permission.

I want fine-grained control over my identity online, and so should you. If you're interested, consider heading to Facebook's feedback center and suggesting that they offer developers better, more specific permissions that ensure we receive only the data we need - and that you want to share.

Tools used

This web app was built with Angular 2 and MeteorJS (united via angular-meteor), and uses angular/material2 and PrimeNG UI components.
